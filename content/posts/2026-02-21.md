For several years, I took no notes. Or rather, I took notes the way one might keep a diary – with good intentions, no consistency, and a vague sense of personal failure. I had tried different softwares : the builtin apple notes, Joplin etc. At some point I had resorted to Neovim and a folder named Notes, which is the kind of solution that feels intellectually rigorous for approximately a week, before the absence of any visual pleasure, image support, or cross-device synchronization quietly kills the mood. 

It was only this year that I stumbled upon Obsidian – and it is the kind of discovery one makes with the mild irritation of someone who has spent years solving a problem with a screwdriver, only to be handed the correct tool and realize it was there all along.

## Syncing your notes with git

The requirements were, in theory, modest. An application that would run on both a laptop and a phone – so that one might consult one's notes on the train, or in the particular dead time of waiting rooms, those small parentheses of modern life where the mind, unoccupied, tends toward either frenetic social media consumption or sometimes productivity. I am comfortable with git. More than comfortable, in fact – it is one of those tools that has become so deeply embedded in one's workflow that its absence feels like a minor amputation. I had attempted, several times, to construct some kind of note-taking repository around it. Each attempt had the same arc: initial enthusiasm, a reasonable folder structure, a first commit with a message like initial setup, and then, progressively, nothing.

With Obsidian, something unexpected happened: it worked. A single plugin – named git – installed in one click, configured in five minutes, and suddenly the vault was syncing with a remote repository with the quiet reliability of something that has no reason to fail and, remarkably, doesn't. It simply worked.

## The zen of coding, the zen of taking notes

There was something else, more difficult to name. A question of habitat, perhaps. I had never felt truly at home in any note-taking application – and home, here, means something precise: the ability to move through text the way one moves through a familiar apartment in the dark, without thinking, without friction, guided entirely by muscle memory. I am a Neovim user. It is almost a personality trait at this point. The motions are no longer conscious acts – they are reflexes. Writing any substantial amount of text without them feels like being handed a fork and asked to eat soup. Possible, technically – but unpleasant. 

Obsidian offered a Vim mode. One click and a few CSS tweaks to adjust the colors and the fonts and I felt home.

## Cross device setup

One must, of course, make concessions. Vim motions on a mobile keyboard occupy the same category as other modern absurdities one learns to quietly let go of – and in the specific context of reading a note on a phone while standing on a platform waiting for a train that is four minutes late, the absence of normal mode is survivable. Obsidian has a mobile application. This is not remarkable in itself – most things have mobile applications, including, presumably, some things that shouldn't. What is remarkable is that the git plugin works there too. The same vault, the same repository, the same notes – available, synchronized, consistent across devices. The thing that I was secretly trying to search for was simply handled.

I should, at this point, tame expectations. The preceding paragraphs may have conveyed an impression of smoothness, of things falling into place with a minimum of resistance. This impression is not entirely accurate. I am an iPhone user – and Apple, in its infinite wisdom, has a particular talent for making reasonable things unnecessarily complicated, in ways that are difficult to explain to people who have never encountered them. Let me show you how I set this up.

On mobile, the Obsidian git plugin requires the use of the http git uri to function properly. We will need to configure the git repository properly, before we set it up on the iphone.

First things first, let's clone the repository:
```bash
git clone git@github.com:tywr/MyVault.git
```

Then we set the url to use https instead of ssh:
```bash
git remote set-url https://github.com/tywr/MyVault.git
```

The solution I found then was this: zip the entire repository – .git metadata included, which is the part that actually matters – and AirDrop it to the phone. One could theoretically use email, or any number of other transfer methods, but there is something faintly appropriate about using Apple's own proprietary proximity-based file transfer protocol to work around Apple's proprietary restrictions. The zip lands on the phone, gets unzipped, and Obsidian can point at it as a vault.

The last step is a GitHub personal access token – generated from the account settings, pasted into the plugin's configuration window on the phone. A small ritual, the kind of thing that takes thirty seconds once you know where to look and twenty-five minutes the first time, spent navigating GitHub's settings interface with the quiet desperation of someone who knows the option exists but cannot, for the life of them, find it.

And then it works. All of it. Across both devices, without further intervention. Then it's up to you to decide if you prefer manually syncing the content to git, or if you want it to happen every X minutes.

## Friction removal

What remains, is something almost embarrassingly simple: an app that opens, contains your notes, and is the same app on every device. The notes you wrote yesterday are there. The ones you wrote on the train are there. Friction, in the specific sense that makes people quietly abandon their own systems, has been removed.

The effects compound in unexpected places. I am learning German – a language that offers, in exchange for considerable grammatical suffering, a certain satisfaction when things eventually click. Vocabulary acquisition, in particular, is a discipline of repetition and consistency, two things that evaporate immediately when the tools get in the way. With a flashcard plugin, sets of cards live as plain markdown files inside the vault, versioned, synchronized, available. The threshold between wanting to study and actually studying collapses to almost nothing. One pulls out the phone — waiting for coffee, standing on a platform, occupying the dead minutes that the day distributes generously and one usually wastes — and the flashcards are simply there. I cannot insist enough on this point: the mere knowledge that one can open the phone and immediately begin is, somehow, more than half the discipline.

## Conclusion

What all of this comes down to, is friction. Not the dramatic kind – not crashes, not data loss, not the catastrophic failures – but the small, chronic, almost imperceptible kind that accumulates in the gap between wanting to do something and actually doing it. The kind that, over months and years, quietly convinces you that you are undisciplined, when in fact you are simply using the wrong tools.

Obsidian understood something that few applications bother to: that people do not want a note-taking app. They want a place. A place that responds to their habits, their workflows, their deeply irrational attachment to particular keyboard motions. Neovim users have known this for a long time – the entire philosophy of the editor is that a tool should eventually disappear, become an extension of thought rather than an obstacle to it, something so tailored to its user that it stops feeling like software and starts feeling like a room one has lived in long enough to navigate blind. Obsidian, quietly and without making too much of it, extended that philosophy to the broader act of thinking and learning.
