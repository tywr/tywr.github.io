For several years, I took no notes. I had tried different software: the built-in Apple Notes, Joplin, etc. At some point I had resorted to Neovim and a folder named Notes, which is the kind of solution that feels appropriate for approximately a week. Then I abandoned it. I was mostly lacking image support and multi-device syncing. It was only this year that I stumbled upon Obsidian – and it is the kind of discovery that one makes with the mild irritation of someone who could have discovered it earlier if one had actually looked for it earlier.

## Syncing your notes with git

The requirements were modest: an application that would run on both a laptop and a phone – so that one might consult one's notes on the train, or in a particular dead time, those small parentheses of modern life where the mind, unoccupied, tends toward either frenetic social media consumption or vague productivity.

As it turns out, there is a famous piece of software made exactly for the purpose of keeping track of files remotely: it's called Git, and most software engineers like me use it every day to version code. I had already attempted, several times, to construct some kind of note-taking repository around it. I never stuck with it.

With Obsidian, something unexpected happened: it worked. A single plugin – named Git – installed in one click, configured in five minutes, and suddenly my notes were synced to a remote repository. It simply worked.

## The zen of coding, the zen of taking notes

There was something else, more difficult to name. A question of habits, perhaps. I had never felt truly at home in any note-taking application – and home, here, means something precise: the ability to move through text the way one moves through a familiar apartment in the dark, without thinking, without friction, guided entirely by muscle memory. I am a Neovim user. It is almost a personality trait at this point. The motions are no longer conscious acts – they are reflexes. Writing any substantial amount of text without them feels like being handed a fork and asked to eat soup. Possible, technically – but unpleasant. 

Obsidian offered a Vim mode. One click, a few CSS tweaks to adjust the colours and the fonts; I felt at home.

## Cross device setup

I agreed to make a few concessions. Vim motions on a mobile keyboard are absurd – in the specific context of reading a note on a phone while standing on a platform waiting for a train that is exactly four minutes late – the absence of normal mode is survivable. Obsidian has a mobile application. This is not remarkable in itself – most things have mobile applications, including some things that shouldn't. What is remarkable is that the git plugin works there too. The same notes, the same repository – available, synchronized, consistent across devices. The thing that I was somehow trying to search for was simply handled.

I should, at this point, tame expectations. The preceding paragraphs may have conveyed an impression of smoothness, of things falling into place with a minimum of resistance. This impression is not entirely accurate. I am an iPhone user – and Apple, in its infinite wisdom, has a particular talent for making reasonable things unnecessarily complicated, in ways that are difficult to explain to people who have never encountered them. Setting up git on the iPhone was not that straightforward; let me explain.

_A small detail before we proceed: the Obsidian Git plugin on mobile requires an HTTP Git URI rather than SSH. It means the repository must be configured accordingly on the laptop side before any of it touches the phone._

_The next steps are done using a terminal._

1. Clone the repository:
```bash
git clone git@github.com:tywr/MyVault.git
```

2. Set the URL to use HTTPS instead of SSH:
```bash
git remote set-url origin https://github.com/tywr/MyVault.git
```

3. Zip the full folder `MyVault` (`.git` metadata included: it is the part that actually matters the most).
4. Send the zip file to your phone (in my case I used AirDrop from my laptop, but one can use another file transfer method, like an email).

The last step is a GitHub personal access token – generated from the account settings, pasted into the plugin's configuration window on the phone.

And then it works. All of it. Across both devices, without further intervention. Then it's up to you to decide if you prefer manually syncing the content to git, or if you want it to happen every X minutes.

## Friction removal

What remains is something surprisingly simple: an app that opens, contains your notes, and is the same app on every device. The notes you wrote yesterday are there. The ones you wrote on the train are there. Friction, in the sense that makes people slowly abandon their own systems, has been removed.

The effects compound in unexpected places. I am learning German – a language that offers, in exchange for considerable grammatical suffering, a certain satisfaction when things eventually click. Vocabulary learning, in particular, is a matter of repetition and consistency, two things that evaporate immediately when friction gets in the way. With a flashcard plugin, sets of cards live as plain markdown files inside the vault, versioned, synchronized, available. The friction between wanting to study and actually studying collapses to almost nothing. One pulls out the phone – waiting for coffee, occupying the boring few minutes that the day distributes generously and one usually wastes – and the flashcards are simply there. I cannot insist enough on this point: the simple knowledge that one can open the phone and immediately begin, almost feels like a subtle invitation to study.

## Conclusion

What all of this comes down to is friction. Not the dramatic kind – not crashes, not data loss, not the big failures – but the small, chronic, almost imperceptible kind that accumulates in the gap between wanting to do something and actually doing it. The kind that, over months and years, insidiously convinces you that you are undisciplined, when in fact you are simply using the wrong tools.

Obsidian understood something that few applications bother to: that people do not want just a note-taking app. They want a place. A place that responds to their habits, their workflows, their irrational attachment to particular keyboard motions. Vim users have known this for a long time – the entire philosophy of the editor is that a tool should eventually disappear, become an extension of thought rather than an obstacle to it, something so tailored to its user that it stops feeling like software and starts feeling like a room one has lived in long enough to navigate blind. Obsidian, in a way, extended that philosophy to the broader act of taking notes and learning.
